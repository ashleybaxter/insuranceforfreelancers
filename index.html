Protect Your Livelihood From As Little As £100 Per Annum

'Business insurance' is an umbrella term for different types of cover. In the UK, the main types of cover are:

  • Professional indemnity
  • Public liability
  • Employers' liability

How Does Business Insurance Affect You As A Freelancer?

Business is unpredictable, but insurance provides a financial buffer to deal with nasty situations. Clients can have faith in your business knowing you'll be able to continue to deliver even if something bad happens.

In fact, in the UK you can't contract for some businesses unless you have business insurance.

Depending on the type of cover you choose, business insurance can protect you from financial loss due to negligent mistakes, copyright or trademark infringement, cases of injury or illness to employees or clients and much more.

What cover you want will depend on the services you offer, so let's break it down.

Professional Indemnity Insurance

Professional indemnity is the most popular cover for freelancers to have. In a nutshell, it's there to protect you in case you make a careless mistake.

You may have seen this referred to as "Oops, I screwed up and now I'm being sued" insurance, which sounds about right!

The biggest cause of claims against freelancers working within design is copyright infringement. Professional indemnity covers legal action levelled against you for copyright or trademark infringement, so it's a popular cover amongst freelance web and graphic designers.

Example: Mixpanel were sued for £1.5 million for font copyright infringement. Read more…

However, professional indemnity covers you for much more than just copyright infringement, such as any negligable mistakes you make that a client claims has caused them loss of income.

Whether it's advice or a service you provide to your client, a client may perceive you to have caused them loss of income. A developer's broken code, for example, or a designer's typo.

Some freelance professions that would benefit from professional indemnity include; consultants, web developers or designers, photographers, illustrators, copywriters.

But I Have A Bulletproof Contract

Yes, you have a contract. It's rock solid and hasn't failed you yet. However, you're not immune from a client taking legal action against you just because you work with a contract, and professional indemnity covers the legal fees, even if you lose the case and are in the wrong.

Public Liability Insurance

Public liability isn't as popular a choice for freelancers as professional indemnity, but depends entirely on where you work from and how often you interact with clients or members of the public.

Basically, if a client is on the property you conduct your business from—even if that's your home—you are now liable for them and their wellbeing. If they suffer injury or illness, you are responsible for that.

This is a scary thought if a client sustains an injury on your property which prevents them from working, requiring compensation from you.

Example: A photographer was sued for £195,000 for breaking a statue. Read more…

Public liability also covers you on client premises. It's not just for injury or illness to people, but also damage to property or possessions. For example, if you damage a client's computer and are liable for the repair cost.

Again, whether you need this depends on where you work from. If you work exclusively from home and deal with all clients remotely, you probably don't need public liability insurance. A photographer, on the other hand, who is on location and interacting with people regularly, would be mistaken to forgo public liability.

Employers' Liability Insurance

Freelancing can often be a one man band operation, but it's not uncommon for freelancers to team up with other people to compliment their skill-set.

For example, a designer may hire a front-end developer to bring their clients' designs to life. Even if it's a temporary or part-time arrangement, you're legally obligated to have employers' liability insurance.

Employers' liability isn't too dissimilar to public liability insurance, covering injury or illness caused to your employees as a result of business activities.

This means any claims from employees who've been injured or become ill as a result of working for you are covered.

Even if you're employing friends and working from home, you're still required by law to have cover of at least £5 million. Each day you go without can incur a hefty fine.

Other Types Of Cover

Those are the three key covers, but other types of business insurance include:

  • Business contents (equipment, office supplies etc)
  • Business interruption (covers loss of income if business temporarily closes due to disaster)
  • Business property (covers physical office premises)

Mix And Match

Because business insurance isn't a 'one size fits all' solution, most insurers offering cover for freelancers enable you to pick and choose which covers you need, all under one policy.
